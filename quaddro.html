Designing for the wrong market

How user research reshaped Quaddro's product strategy and helped the company pivot from the U.S. to Brazil.

Quaddro was originally built for the U.S. market. When Brazil emerged as a promising direction, we first needed to answer a more fundamental question: would the same product work for Brazilian entrepreneurs?

We quickly realised we were not redesigning an interface. We were challenging the assumptions behind the product itself.

My role

Turning research into company direction

I partnered directly with the founders to understand the Brazilian market and translate research into product strategy. I led the discovery process end to end, from planning and interviews to synthesis, product principles, MVP definition and interface design.

The work crossed research, facilitation and execution. I aligned the founders around the product opportunity, defined what the first release should prove, and collaborated with Product, Engineering and QA to turn those decisions into a mobile-first experience.

The unknowns

We started with uncertainty, not features

Before making product decisions, we identified the questions that could change the direction of the company.

  1. 01Who are Brazilian micro-entrepreneurs?
  2. 02How do they actually manage their business today?
  3. 03Which assumptions from the U.S. product no longer hold?
  4. 04What would users need on day one?
  5. 05What should we intentionally leave out?
The risk was not building the wrong interface. It was solving the wrong problem.
Research

Research that changed everything

Rather than validate the existing product, we used interviews and observation sessions to understand how Brazilian entrepreneurs actually worked. A completely different picture emerged.

The entrepreneurs we met were not using CRMs, managing detailed spreadsheets or looking for advanced business software. They built their businesses around tools they already trusted, stitching together simple routines throughout the day.

The biggest insight was not about technology. It was about behaviour. People did not need more features. They needed a product that respected the way they already worked.

Strategy

Five assumptions became five product decisions

The research did not simply improve the interface. It changed the product strategy and became the foundation of the MVP.

More features create more valueSimplicity increases confidenceRemove unnecessary complexity

Users will adapt to the productThe product must adapt to usersMirror familiar behaviours instead of creating new ones

The MVP

The smallest product that could validate the direction

Every design decision reinforced one objective: reduce friction for people already overwhelmed by running a business. We focused the MVP on fast invoicing, Pix-ready payments, simple customer management, clear language and lightweight performance for lower-end Android devices.

Fast

Immediate value before explanation.

Familiar

Existing behaviours before new mental models.

Light

Essential workflows before feature depth.

Instead of introducing new behaviours, we designed around existing ones. The product became a bridge between the routines entrepreneurs already trusted and the control they needed to grow.

Business impact

From adapting a product to defining a market

The redesign helped transform Quaddro from a product designed for the U.S. into one built around Brazilian entrepreneurs. The work supported the company's pivot, established the product foundation for future growth, and contributed to the product evolution behind a R$16M funding round.

Reflection

Question assumptions before proposing solutions

This project fundamentally changed the way I think about Product Design. When a product enters a new market, the biggest risk is not building the wrong interface. It is solving the wrong problem.

Since then, I have approached discovery by questioning assumptions before proposing solutions. Whether working at a startup or a marketplace serving millions of users, that principle continues to guide how I help teams make better product decisions.
