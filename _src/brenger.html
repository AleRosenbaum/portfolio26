Ale Rosenbaum Case study · 2024

Integrating Brenger into Marktplaats, one step at a time

17.3 million large-item ads listed every year with no way to ship them. This is how a discovery on user needs became Marktplaats's first integrated large-item delivery service, built iteratively from a chat banner to a full transactional flow.

As the Product Designer on the Shipping & Messaging team, I led the design end to end: I shaped the iterative strategy with the PM, ran the foundational research (n=1,000), designed every phase from promotion to full integration, and coordinated usability testing.

In 2023, 4 of the top 10 most searched items on Marktplaats were large items with no shipping option.

Existing carriers maxed out at 23kg and 176cm. Sofas, dining tables, and washing machines simply didn't fit. Meanwhile, Brenger was already handling 25,000+ Marktplaats-attributed shipments per year without any product integration.

Research

Trust, not price, was the real barrier

A survey of 1,000 buyers and sellers revealed that 75% picked up with their own car because no viable alternative existed. The main reason buyers wouldn't ship? They needed to see the item in person first (38%). Price expectations were realistic — 62% expected €0–40 for a €300 sofa. Sellers wanted easier delivery (40%) and more reach (32%). Sizing the opportunity: 1.6M ads had potential for paid shipment.

how might we help buyers and sellers trade large items in a safe and convenient way?
Process

Build the skateboard, not the moving truck

I facilitated a cross-functional discovery with design, product, data, and engineering. The iterative model was deliberate: each phase would deliver user value, validate assumptions, and earn the right to the next investment. Three steps — Skateboard, Electric Step, Electric Bike — each unlocking more integration depth.

Decisions

Curbside delivery first: 72% of Marktplaats-attributed Brenger shipments were curbside, so we scoped the MVP to fixed-price curbside only — simpler pricing, fewer inputs, faster to build. Address as the hardest UX problem: I ran a dedicated ideation on collecting addresses without disrupting the flow. Winner: reuse the address book and pre-fill from profile data.

Phase 1 — Skateboard

Testing desirability with in-chat promotion

Live from November 2023 in three L1 categories (Home & Decor, Garden & Patio, White Goods). A banner inside the Marktplaats chat told users they could ship large items with Brenger, then redirected them to Brenger's platform to complete the order.

No backend integration. No payment inside Marktplaats. Just enough to measure intent without engineering investment.

An exit survey (n=1,871) validated demand: half considered using the service. Signal strong enough to invest in real integration.

Phase 2 — Electric Step

Item paid at Marktplaats, shipping completed at Brenger

The first real integration: Brenger appeared as a shipping option alongside "Self Shipping" in the payment proposal. Buyers paid for the item inside Marktplaats, then were redirected to Brenger to complete shipping details and payment. Curbside-only pricing, estimated via the Brenger API based on distance.

The split introduced friction — two payment moments, two platforms — but it validated the full transaction loop. Users could now complete an item purchase on Marktplaats and have it delivered, with Buyer Protection covering the entire trade.

Phase 3 — Electric Bike

Full payment on Marktplaats, scheduling via Brenger

The biggest friction point removed: users paid for both item and shipping in a single Marktplaats checkout. Post-purchase, Brenger handled appointment scheduling. The platform could now offer better prices than going to Brenger directly, and Buyer Protection covered the entire transaction end to end.

Curbside remained the default (72% of shipments), keeping the flow simple: distance and item dimensions determined the price. No floor-level questions, no elevator inputs. For door-to-door delivery, users could upgrade at Brenger post-purchase.

From zero shipping options for large items to a fully integrated flow in three iterations.
The solution

One checkout. Trusted delivery. Buyer Protection included.

Sellers create a payment proposal with Brenger as the shipping method, buyers see a transparent total (item + transport), pay in one step, and both parties schedule the pickup. The platform's new principle: if it fits in a box, use PostNL. If it needs a courier, use Brenger.

Results

From 0 to 300 weekly shipments. A new revenue line for Marktplaats.

Each phase validated the next investment. The skateboard proved demand, the electric step proved the transaction model, and the electric bike proved the business case.

The financial model projects €1.7M revenue for 2025 and €3.5M by 2026. The integration positions Marktplaats as the only major Dutch marketplace with integrated large-item shipping — a moat no direct competitor currently offers.

Reflection

Defining the product in increments

The iterative approach was the design decision. Phase 1 asked "is there demand?", Phase 2 asked "will people complete the transaction?", Phase 3 asked "can we make it convenient enough to scale?" Without that structure, we'd have built a full integration against unvalidated assumptions.

What I'd do differently: map backend constraints earlier. The address collection problem surfaced late and required a dedicated ideation that could have been run in discovery.
